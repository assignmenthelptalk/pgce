What Is a PGCE Subject Knowledge Audit?

A PGCE Subject Knowledge Audit (SKA) is a structured self-evaluation in which the trainee assesses their own knowledge and understanding across the subjects or curriculum areas they are training to teach. For [PGCE Secondary](/pgce-secondary-assignment-help/) trainees, this means the specialist subject — Mathematics, English, History, Science, and so on — assessed against the National Curriculum requirements and Ofsted subject inspection frameworks. For [PGCE Primary](/pgce-primary-assignment-help/) trainees, the audit typically covers all core subjects (English, Mathematics, and Science) and may extend to foundation subjects.

The SKA is assessed differently across PGCE providers. Some treat it as a formative, non-assessed activity. Others require a written submission with an accompanying development plan — a formal academic document that must demonstrate critical self-awareness, evidence-based target-setting, and explicit connection to Teachers' Standards 2 (Promote good progress and outcomes) and 3 (Demonstrate good subject and curriculum knowledge). It is the TS3 submission that most directly assesses this — requiring not just knowledge of curriculum content, but Shulman's (1986) pedagogical content knowledge (PCK): the ability to represent subject knowledge in ways that make it accessible to learners.

Where the SKA requires a written document, it typically includes: a self-evaluation section (rating knowledge across curriculum topics); a reflection section (analysing the implications of gaps); and a development plan (targets, resources, and timelines for addressing gaps).

How to Structure the Written Sections of a PGCE Subject Knowledge Audit

If your PGCE provider requires a written SKA submission, the document typically has three structural sections. Each has different academic requirements and different common failure modes.

Self-Evaluation Grid. A topic-by-topic rating of subject knowledge (typically: confident/secure; developing; needs development). Most providers supply the topic grid — you complete it against the curriculum. The critical error at this stage: rating yourself as "confident" across all areas to avoid looking inadequate. Assessors expect honest identification of gaps — the SKA is a professional development tool, not a performance review. A uniformly "confident" SKA signals either dishonesty or lack of self-awareness, both of which are concerning in a trainee teacher. Identify genuine gaps: these will form the basis of your development plan, which is where academic analysis is required.

Written Reflection. This is the analytical section — and the section most trainees underwrite. The reflection must move beyond describing the gaps to analysing their pedagogical implications. "I have limited knowledge of the Norman Conquest beyond basic facts" is a content gap identification. "My limited knowledge of the Norman Conquest means I do not yet have a secure enough understanding of historiographical debate around Anglo-Saxon resistance to design tasks that require learners to evaluate competing interpretations — a pedagogical gap that connects to TS3 (subject and curriculum knowledge) and to Counsell's (2018) disciplinary knowledge framework for History" is a pedagogical knowledge gap analysis at Level 7.

Development Plan. Targets must be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound), connected to named resources, and linked to Teachers' Standards. Vague targets ("I will improve my History knowledge") do not meet academic requirements. Specific targets ("I will read Bartlett and Burton (2012) Medieval England before the start of spring term placement, focusing on chapters 3–5 covering Anglo-Norman governance, and complete the corresponding self-assessment quiz to benchmark my improvement against the TS3 self-evaluation grid") demonstrate the planning rigour that PGCE assessors look for.

Self-Evaluating Subject Knowledge Gaps: What Tutors Want to See

PGCE tutors evaluating a subject knowledge audit want to see honest, analytically grounded self-assessment — not a performance of competence. The purpose of the SKA is to identify genuine gaps so that the training year can address them systematically. A trainee who presents a uniformly strong self-evaluation cannot justify a meaningful development plan, and assessors will note the inconsistency.

What constitutes a meaningful gap in the context of a PGCE SKA: not merely a content gap (areas of the curriculum the trainee does not know well) but a pedagogical knowledge gap (aspects of the curriculum the trainee knows but cannot yet teach effectively). Shulman (1986, 1987) coined "pedagogical content knowledge" (PCK) to describe this — the specific knowledge teachers need to represent subject content in accessible, accurate, and engaging ways. PCK includes: knowledge of common learner misconceptions in the subject; knowledge of the most effective representations and analogies for specific concepts; knowledge of the sequence in which concepts are best introduced.

Example of PCK gap identification for a Maths trainee: "I am secure in my own knowledge of fractions operations (content knowledge — TS3). However, I have limited awareness of the specific misconceptions that KS3 learners typically hold about equivalent fractions — for example, the common error of adding numerators and denominators separately (1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5). I do not yet know the sequence of representations (concrete, pictorial, abstract — Bruner, 1960) that most effectively develops the concept from KS2 fractions into KS3 ratio. This pedagogical knowledge gap will be addressed through Ryan and Williams (2007) Children's Mathematics 4–15, which catalogues common misconceptions with recommended teaching sequences."

PGCE Subject Knowledge Development Plan: How to Write Targets and Timelines

A subject knowledge development plan is a forward-looking document that specifies how identified gaps will be addressed during the training year. It must be realistic, specific, and time-bound — and it must acknowledge that some gaps will be addressed through placement teaching experience, not just independent study.

Resources that appear in PGCE subject knowledge development plans: curriculum subject association resources (e.g., the Mathematical Association; the Historical Association; the National Association for the Teaching of English — NATE); subject-specific academic texts (not generic pedagogy texts — the development plan should reference subject-specialist reading); Ofsted subject inspection reports (published for each subject following research reviews — these identify the most significant subject knowledge and pedagogical knowledge gaps nationally, which provides context for individual gap identification); and deliberate practice on placement (planning lessons on topics where knowledge is less secure, seeking mentor feedback specifically on subject knowledge accuracy).

A well-structured development target: "Gap: Limited knowledge of Romantic period poetry beyond Keats and Shelley (GCSE curriculum coverage). Pedagogical implication: unable to design comparative tasks that require learners to evaluate Romantic poets' contrasting responses to nature, which is a common GCSE and A-level question type. Action: Read Beer (1990) Romantic Consciousness (chapters on Blake, Wordsworth, and Coleridge) before half-term in autumn term. Teach one lesson on Blake during placement weeks 3–5, video-reviewing my subject knowledge accuracy with my mentor. Success measure: confident self-rating on Romantic poetry by the end of autumn term placement, confirmed by mentor observation feedback." Timeline, resource, and success measure are all specified.

Teachers' Standards 2 and 3 in Subject Knowledge Audits

TS3 — Demonstrate good subject and curriculum knowledge — is the primary standard in subject knowledge audits. Its sub-statements include: having a secure knowledge of the relevant subject(s) and curriculum areas; demonstrating a critical understanding of developments in the subject and curriculum areas; and demonstrating an understanding of and taking responsibility for promoting high standards of literacy, articulacy, and the correct use of standard English, whatever the teacher's specialist subject.

TS2 — Promote good progress and outcomes by pupils — connects to the SKA through the teacher's ability to plan progression: "to demonstrate knowledge and understanding of how pupils learn and to make this work well in the classroom." Understanding how learners develop through the curriculum — including the common misconceptions they encounter at each stage — is both a TS2 requirement and a PCK requirement. Audits that address both TS2 (how pupils learn the subject progressively) and TS3 (what the teacher knows about the subject) are more analytically complete than those that address only curriculum content knowledge.

TS8 — Fulfil wider professional responsibilities — is relevant to the development plan component: "taking responsibility for improving teaching through appropriate professional development, responding to advice and feedback from colleagues." The development plan is a TS8 document — it demonstrates that the trainee takes responsibility for their own continuing professional development and has a systematic plan for addressing identified gaps.

PGCE Maths Subject Knowledge Audit Help

For [PGCE Secondary Maths](/pgce-secondary-maths-assignment-help/) trainees, the SKA typically covers the full GCSE and A-level Mathematics curriculum. Common areas where trainees identify pedagogical gaps — not content gaps — include: teaching algebraic reasoning as a transition from arithmetic (the relationship between procedural fluency and conceptual understanding in algebra); the teaching of fractions, ratio, and proportion in relation to common KS3 and KS4 misconceptions; statistics and data handling — many trainees are secure in statistical calculation but have limited knowledge of how to teach statistical reasoning as opposed to procedure; and calculus at A-level, where many trainees have content knowledge but limited experience of how to develop intuitive understanding of limits and continuity in learners new to the concept.

Key resources for Maths SKA development plans: Ryan, J. and Williams, J. (2007) Children's Mathematics 4–15: Learning from Errors and Misconceptions; Swan, M. (2006) Collaborative Learning in Mathematics; the NCETM (National Centre for Excellence in the Teaching of Mathematics) curriculum framework and professional development resources.

PGCE English Subject Knowledge Audit Help

For [PGCE Secondary English](/pgce-secondary-english-assignment-help/) and Primary trainees, English SKA gaps most commonly appear in: grammar and linguistic knowledge (many trainees have strong intuitive grammatical competence but cannot name or explain grammatical structures explicitly — required for teaching grammar explicitly under the National Curriculum); reading theory (the Simple View of Reading — Gough and Tunmer, 1986 — underpins the phonics-first approach embedded in the primary curriculum and Ofsted's research review; Secondary English trainees need to be able to engage with this); literary criticism and genre knowledge (trainees may know texts well but have limited knowledge of the critical discourse surrounding them — important for GCSE and A-level English Literature); and media texts and media literacy (an under-prepared area for many trainees).

Frequently Asked Questions

How honest should I be in a PGCE subject knowledge audit?

Fully honest. The subject knowledge audit is a professional development tool — its purpose is to identify gaps so they can be addressed. PGCE tutors and mentors expect to see genuine self-awareness about areas for development; they are not evaluating your current knowledge level but your ability to identify and plan for improvement. A trainee who presents an unrealistically positive self-evaluation not only produces a weaker academic document but also fails to engage with the professional development purpose of the audit. Identifying a gap and demonstrating a credible plan to address it is what the SKA is designed to produce.

What is pedagogical content knowledge and why does it matter for the SKA?

Pedagogical content knowledge (PCK) — coined by Shulman (1986, 1987) — is the knowledge a teacher needs to represent subject content in ways that are accessible, accurate, and engaging for learners of a particular age and ability. It includes knowledge of common misconceptions, effective representations and analogies, and appropriate sequencing. It differs from content knowledge (knowing the subject) and pedagogical knowledge (knowing how to teach in general). For PGCE SKAs, PCK gaps — "I know this topic but I do not know how to teach it effectively" — are more analytically valuable than content gaps alone, because they connect directly to what PGCE training is designed to develop.

Do I need to reference academic texts in a subject knowledge audit?

If the SKA is assessed as an academic document, yes — the written reflection and development plan sections should reference relevant academic and professional sources (Shulman, 1986; subject association frameworks; subject-specific academic texts). If the SKA is a formative, non-assessed professional development tool, formal academic referencing may not be required — check your programme handbook. When in doubt, including references demonstrates academic seriousness and is unlikely to be penalised.
