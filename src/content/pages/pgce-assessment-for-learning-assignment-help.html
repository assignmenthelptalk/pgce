What Is an Assessment for Learning (AfL) Assignment in PGCE?

An Assessment for Learning assignment requires PGCE trainees to write academically about formative assessment — the ongoing, in-lesson process of gathering evidence about learner understanding and using it to adapt teaching. At Level 7, this means engaging critically with the research base behind AfL, evaluating formative assessment strategies used in placement lessons, and evidencing Teachers' Standards 4 (plan and teach well-structured lessons) and 6 (make accurate and productive use of assessment).

AfL assignments appear across all PGCE routes — [Primary](/pgce-primary-assignment-help/), [Secondary](/pgce-secondary-assignment-help/), and [Further Education](/pgce-further-education-assignment-help/) — because formative assessment is a universal pedagogical requirement. The theoretical basis is primarily Black and Wiliam's (1998) landmark research Inside the Black Box, supplemented by Hattie's (2009) Visible Learning meta-analysis, Wiliam's (2011) Embedded Formative Assessment, and Dylan Wiliam's five strategies for AfL. Assignments that engage only with generic descriptions of "questioning techniques" or "exit tickets" without connecting to this research base will not meet Level 7 marking criteria.

The most common assignment structure: analyse two or three formative assessment strategies used in a placement unit of work; evaluate their effectiveness using pupil response data (exit tickets, live marking, questioning, mini-whiteboards); apply AfL theory to explain what worked and what failed; connect to TS4 and TS6; propose improvements based on research evidence. Word count typically 2,500–4,000 words.

Black and Wiliam's Inside the Black Box: How to Use This in PGCE Assignments

Black, P. and Wiliam, D. (1998) Inside the Black Box: Raising Standards Through Classroom Assessment — published as a review of 250 research studies — is the foundational text for AfL in PGCE academic writing. Its key findings are: formative assessment is one of the most powerful interventions available to raise pupil achievement; most classroom assessment practices are summative rather than formative; effective feedback focuses on the work, not the person; and learners need to understand the criteria for quality before they can self-assess effectively.

How to use Inside the Black Box in PGCE assignments: cite it with the correct date (1998) and authors, and apply its specific claims to your placement evidence. Do not use it as a general definition of "AfL" — engage with its research findings. "Black and Wiliam (1998) demonstrated that feedback which identifies the gap between current performance and the learning goal is more effective than marks or grades alone — a finding that challenges my practice of marking books with numerical scores without written commentary during placement."

Common errors: citing "Black and Wiliam (1998)" without engaging with its specific claims; confusing Inside the Black Box (1998) with Wiliam's later solo publications (2011, 2016); or treating the text as providing prescriptive AfL strategies (it does not — it identifies principles supported by research, not a list of classroom techniques to follow).

Wiliam's five key strategies for embedded formative assessment (Wiliam, 2011): (1) Clarifying, sharing, and understanding learning intentions and success criteria; (2) Engineering effective classroom discussions and learning tasks that elicit evidence of learning; (3) Providing feedback that moves learners forward; (4) Activating students as instructional resources for one another (peer assessment); (5) Activating students as owners of their own learning (self-assessment). Each strategy maps clearly to TS4 and TS6 — use this mapping explicitly in assignments.

Hattie's Feedback Research for PGCE Assignment Writing

Hattie, J. (2009) Visible Learning: A Synthesis of Over 800 Meta-Analyses Relating to Achievement — is the largest meta-analysis of educational research ever conducted. Its headline finding: feedback has one of the highest effect sizes on pupil achievement of any educational intervention (effect size d = 0.73). But Hattie's feedback research is more nuanced than this headline suggests, and PGCE assignments that engage with the nuance score at a higher level.

Hattie and Timperley's (2007) feedback model (published in Review of Educational Research) identifies four levels of feedback: (1) Task level (feedback about the correctness of the task — "this is wrong"); (2) Process level (feedback about strategies used — "your approach to structuring this argument could be improved by…"); (3) Self-regulation level (feedback that develops metacognitive awareness — "how did you decide which evidence to use here?"); (4) Self level (feedback about the person rather than the work — "you are a strong writer"). Hattie and Timperley found that task-level and process-level feedback are most effective for learning; self-level feedback is least effective and can undermine intrinsic motivation.

Application in a PGCE AfL assignment: "My written feedback during placement relied primarily on task-level commentary — 'include more evidence' — rather than process-level guidance — 'your analytical paragraph would be stronger if you introduced the counter-argument before your own position, because this structure demonstrates critical engagement.' Hattie and Timperley's (2007) feedback model suggests that process-level feedback develops the strategic knowledge learners need to independently improve their work, whereas task-level feedback requires the learner to reattempt the task without guidance on how — a limitation that connects to TS6 (making accurate and productive use of assessment)."

Formative vs Summative Assessment in PGCE: What Assignments Must Address

Formative assessment (Assessment FOR Learning) gathers evidence of learning during the learning process and uses it to adapt teaching. Summative assessment (Assessment OF Learning) measures what has been learned at the end of a unit, term, or year. Both appear in PGCE assignments — but AfL assignments focus specifically on formative strategies and the teacher's use of evidence to adapt teaching in real time.

Common formative assessment techniques that appear in PGCE assignment analysis: questioning (Bloom's Taxonomy — revised Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001 — provides a framework for designing questions that target different cognitive levels); exit tickets (brief written tasks completed in the final 5 minutes of a lesson to surface misconceptions); mini-whiteboards (visible, simultaneous responses from all learners — enables the teacher to see the whole class's understanding at once); traffic light self-assessment (learners rate their own understanding — connects to Wiliam's Strategy 5); peer marking (connects to Wiliam's Strategy 4); live marking during independent work (teacher circulates and provides immediate written feedback — connects to Hattie's process-level feedback).

At Level 7, the analysis must go beyond describing the technique to evaluating its effectiveness in the specific placement context. "The exit ticket I used at the end of the fractions lesson revealed that 7 of 24 learners could not correctly identify equivalent fractions — a misconception I had not anticipated and had not addressed during the lesson. This is consistent with Black and Wiliam's (1998) finding that teachers systematically underestimate the proportion of learners who have not understood the lesson objective when relying on volunteered verbal responses rather than systematic written evidence."

How to Critically Evaluate AfL Strategies in Your Own Classroom

Critical evaluation of AfL strategies requires honesty about what did not work and why — not just a celebration of successful formative assessment practice. Assessors at Level 7 specifically look for evidence that the trainee can identify the gap between AfL intention and AfL reality in their own teaching.

Common AfL implementation failures that make strong assignment material: using questioning that only elicits responses from confident, high-attaining pupils (a problem Black and Wiliam identified in 1998 — hands-up questioning samples, not surveys, classroom understanding); providing written feedback that learners do not read or act on ("triple impact marking" — teacher feedback, pupil response, teacher acknowledgment — addresses this but is time-intensive); sharing success criteria as a list of requirements rather than as a model of quality (Shirley Clarke's research on success criteria suggests learners need to see quality exemplified, not just described).

An analytically strong critical evaluation: "My questioning strategy during the fractions unit relied exclusively on volunteered responses — hands-up from self-selecting pupils. Black and Wiliam (1998) specifically identify this as a source of systematic bias in teacher assessment, because volunteers are disproportionately confident and high-attaining. The responses I received created a false impression of class-wide understanding that was contradicted by the exit ticket data at the end of the lesson. In future placements, I will use cold-calling with thinking time and mini-whiteboards to gather representative evidence from all learners — strategies consistent with Wiliam's (2011) Strategy 2 (engineering tasks that elicit evidence of learning from all learners)."

Teachers' Standards 4 and 6: Evidencing Assessment in PGCE Assignments

TS4 — Plan and teach well-structured lessons — includes sub-statement TS4b: promote a love of learning and children's intellectual curiosity. Formative assessment strategies that create intellectual dialogue (open questioning, peer discussion, metacognitive prompts) connect to TS4b as well as TS6. Assignments that recognise this connection — rather than treating TS4 and TS6 as wholly separate — demonstrate the integrative thinking that Level 7 requires.

TS6 — Make accurate and productive use of assessment — includes: knowing and understanding how to assess the relevant subject and curriculum areas; making use of formative and summative assessment to secure pupils' progress; using relevant data to monitor progress, set targets, and plan subsequent lessons; giving pupils regular feedback, both orally and through accurate marking, and encouraging pupils to respond to the feedback. Each sub-component of TS6 can be evidenced separately in an AfL assignment — which provides the trainee with a structural scaffold for the analytical sections.

Pupil Progress Data in PGCE Assignments: What to Include and How

Pupil progress data — assessment results, exit ticket responses, quiz scores, written work samples — is the evidence base for AfL analysis in PGCE assignments. Including data makes the analysis concrete and specific; omitting it leaves the assignment reliant on impressionistic accounts of learning that cannot be verified.

How to include data appropriately: anonymise all pupil information (refer to Pupil A, Pupil B, or cohort-level percentages); use aggregated data where possible ("17 of 24 learners correctly identified the main clause" rather than named pupil results); connect the data to the formative assessment strategy used; analyse what the data reveals about the teaching ("the 30% who could not identify the main clause despite the explanation suggests the worked example I provided was not sufficiently explicit — consistent with Rosenshine's (2012) Principle 2 on presenting new material in small steps").

Frequently Asked Questions

Is formative assessment the same as Assessment for Learning?

Assessment for Learning (AfL) and formative assessment are used interchangeably in most educational literature — both refer to assessment that happens during the learning process and feeds back into teaching decisions. The term "Assessment for Learning" was promoted by Black and Wiliam (1998) specifically to contrast with "Assessment of Learning" (summative assessment). Dylan Wiliam has since preferred the term "formative assessment" for academic precision. In PGCE assignments, both terms are acceptable — use whichever your assignment brief employs, and define it clearly in your introduction.

How much pupil data should I include in an AfL assignment?

Enough to substantiate your analytical claims — not so much that the assignment becomes a data report. Typically: two or three specific data examples (e.g., exit ticket results from a specific lesson, a quiz score distribution, a sample of written feedback given), used as evidence for the analytical argument. Quantitative data (percentages, score distributions) is more compelling than impressionistic accounts ("most pupils seemed to understand"). But data is supporting evidence for your analysis — not a substitute for theoretical engagement.

Which is more important in a PGCE AfL assignment — theory or practice?

Both are required and must be integrated. Practice without theory is anecdote; theory without practice is a literature review. Level 7 assessment requires the candidate to bridge the two — applying named research (Black and Wiliam, 1998; Hattie and Timperley, 2007; Wiliam, 2011) to specific placement evidence, with explicit connections to Teachers' Standards. Assignments that cover theory in one section and practice in another, without integrating them, will not achieve Merit or above.
