PGCE English Subject Pedagogy: What Assignments Cover

PGCE Secondary English assignments cover a distinct body of professional knowledge: the subject-specific theories, research, and curriculum frameworks that govern how English is taught in secondary schools. English subject pedagogy assignments are different from general PGCE assignments — they require engagement not just with generic learning theory (Vygotsky, Bloom) but with English-specific research: reading theory, writing pedagogy, grammar teaching research, literary studies, and the History of English as a school subject.

The primary Teachers' Standard for PGCE Secondary English assignments is TS3 — Demonstrate good subject and curriculum knowledge — interpreted through Shulman's (1986) concept of pedagogical content knowledge (PCK): what English teachers need to know specifically about how to teach reading, writing, grammar, speaking, and literature, not just what they know about English as a discipline. PGCE English assignments that engage only with generic pedagogy (Vygotsky, Rosenshine) without English-specific subject knowledge fail to meet the TS3 requirement at the level expected.

Common assignment formats: subject pedagogy essays (critically evaluating how a specific area of English — reading, writing, grammar — should be taught, with reference to research); subject knowledge audits (see [PGCE subject knowledge audit help](/pgce-subject-knowledge-audit-help/)); [lesson study assignments](/pgce-lesson-study-assignment-help/) focused on a specific English teaching challenge; and [curriculum design essays](/pgce-curriculum-design-assignment-help/) evaluating the English department's medium-term planning against curriculum theory. Word counts are typically 2,500–4,000 words.

Reading Theory in PGCE English Assignments: The Simple View and the Rose Report

Reading theory is the most heavily assessed English-specific content area in PGCE Secondary (and Primary) English assignments. The Simple View of Reading (Gough and Tunmer, 1986) proposes that reading comprehension is the product of two components: word recognition (decoding — the ability to read printed words accurately) and language comprehension (the ability to understand spoken language). Reading Comprehension = Decoding × Language Comprehension. Neither component alone is sufficient — a child who can decode but cannot comprehend spoken language will not understand what they read; a child who comprehends spoken language but cannot decode cannot access text independently.

The Simple View of Reading became UK government policy through the Rose Review (Rose, 2006) — the government-commissioned review of early reading that concluded systematic synthetic phonics should be the primary method of teaching reading in Primary schools. The Rose Report is the policy context for phonics teaching in England; Ofsted's research review for English (Ofsted, 2022) reaffirms this position and extends it to secondary literacy development.

For PGCE Secondary English assignments: the Simple View of Reading explains why secondary-age learners with reading difficulties often have deficits in one or both components — some can decode but struggle with academic vocabulary and text comprehension; others may have reasonable language comprehension but struggle with fluency. This framework informs differentiation decisions (TS5) and supports analysis of how English departments' reading programmes address both components.

Academic engagement with reading theory at Level 7: do not simply cite the Simple View as an uncontested truth. Engage with critiques — Seidenberg (2017) argues that the Simple View underplays the role of orthographic knowledge and fluency; the Reading Wars debate between phonics-first and whole-language approaches (Adams, 1990; Goodman, 1970) provides a historical context for the current phonics consensus and its contested status. Assignments that engage with the tension in the research literature — rather than presenting the Simple View as settled — demonstrate Level 7 critical engagement.

Writing Theory for PGCE English: Process Approach vs Genre Theory

Two contrasting frameworks dominate English writing pedagogy and PGCE English assignments: the process approach and genre theory. Each has a different view of what writing is, how it should be taught, and what development looks like — and the tension between them is productively controversial at Level 7.

The process approach (Graves, 1983; Murray, 1980; Elbow, 1973): writing is a recursive, meaning-making process — not a product to be corrected. Effective writing teaching focuses on the process of drafting, revising, and editing; on authorial choice and creative agency; and on the development of a writer's voice. Process approaches favour extended writing, peer response, and workshop environments. They are most influential in creative writing pedagogy and in UK secondary English teaching traditions that emphasise literary response and personal writing.

Genre theory (Cope & Kalantzis, 1993; Martin, 1989 — the Australian genre movement; Myhill, 2011 — UK grammar-for-writing research): writing is a socially situated practice — the ability to write effectively requires knowledge of the genre conventions that govern different text types in different social contexts. Effective writing teaching makes genre conventions explicit, models the features of target text types, and provides guided practice within specific genres. Genre approaches are most influential in academic writing pedagogy, functional literacy programmes, and in UK secondary English teaching traditions shaped by GCSE Assessment Objectives (AQA, Edexcel, OCR).

PGCE English assignments that evaluate writing pedagogy must engage with both frameworks and evaluate which is more appropriate for specific learning contexts — rather than positioning one as definitively superior. "The process approach develops authorial agency and creativity but may disadvantage learners who lack tacit knowledge of genre conventions — a gap that is disproportionately distributed by social class (Myhill, 2011 on social contexts of writing). Genre-explicit teaching addresses this gap but may constrain creative expression and reduce learner motivation for extended writing. For my Year 10 class writing argumentative essays for GCSE, a hybrid approach — explicit genre instruction followed by process-oriented drafting and peer response — was most appropriate."

Grammar Teaching in PGCE English Assignments: What Research Says

Grammar teaching is one of the most contested areas of English pedagogy — and a high-value topic for PGCE English assignments because it sits at the intersection of subject knowledge, pedagogical knowledge, and policy. The National Curriculum (DfE, 2014) requires explicit grammar teaching from primary school through GCSE; Ofsted's research review for English (2022) supports grammar teaching as part of a knowledge-rich English curriculum. But the evidence base is complex.

Andrews et al. (2004) — a systematic review commissioned by the English Review Group — found no reliable evidence that formal grammar instruction improved the quality of secondary pupils' writing. This finding challenged the grammar-for-writing tradition and caused significant debate. Myhill et al. (2012) subsequently conducted a randomised controlled trial (Grammar for Writing) that found contextualised grammar teaching — introducing grammatical concepts within the writing process, with a focus on effect and authorial choice rather than decontextualised labelling — did improve writing quality for secondary pupils.

The distinction for PGCE assignments: decontextualised grammar instruction (identifying parts of speech in isolation, labelling sentences) has weak evidence of impact on writing quality. Contextualised grammar instruction (examining how specific grammatical choices create specific effects, then applying those choices in writing) has stronger evidence of impact. "Applying Myhill et al. (2012) — the contextualised grammar teaching approach informed my decision to introduce noun phrase expansion through analysis of its effects in a published text (authorial effect) before asking learners to apply the construction in their own writing (TS4 — well-structured lesson; TS3 — subject knowledge of grammar pedagogy)."

Literature and the Canon in PGCE English Curriculum Essays

Curriculum theory in PGCE English assignments frequently addresses the question of literary canon — which texts are selected for study, whose voices are represented, and what criteria govern selection. For [PGCE Secondary English](/pgce-secondary-assignment-help/) trainees writing curriculum essays, this is both a theoretical and a practical question: schools choose texts, and those choices embed values about what counts as Literature.

Young's (2008) Powerful Knowledge framework — see [PGCE curriculum design assignment help](/pgce-curriculum-design-assignment-help/) — provides a rationale for canonical texts: they contain the concentrated cultural knowledge that gives learners access to literary and intellectual traditions. The counter-argument, articulated by Youdell (2011) and Reay (2012) in the context of cultural reproduction, is that canonical texts often reflect and perpetuate dominant cultural norms, marginalising the literary traditions of communities not represented in the Western academic canon.

Practical application in curriculum essays: evaluate your placement school's or department's text selection against both positions. Does the curriculum include texts that represent diverse voices and experiences? Are canonical texts selected for the richness of their teachable knowledge or for traditional prestige? This is a live debate in English departments across England — and one that PGCE trainees are expected to engage with critically at Level 7, not resolve prescriptively.

PGCE English Subject Knowledge Audit: What to Include

The PGCE Secondary English [subject knowledge audit](/pgce-subject-knowledge-audit-help/) covers: grammar and linguistics (explicit knowledge of grammatical structures and terminology — required for teaching grammar under the National Curriculum); reading theory (the Simple View, phonics development, vocabulary acquisition, comprehension strategies); writing pedagogy (the process approach, genre theory, academic writing conventions); literary knowledge (primary texts studied at GCSE and A-level, critical approaches, context knowledge); spoken language and oracy (dialogic teaching — Mercer & Littleton, 2007 — drama in English, formal spoken language assessment at GCSE); and media and digital literacies.

The most common gap in PGCE English trainee SKAs: grammar knowledge. Many English graduates have strong intuitive grammatical competence — they write well — but limited explicit knowledge of grammatical structures and metalanguage. Explicit grammatical knowledge is required for teaching grammar under the National Curriculum at all key stages, and for preparing GCSE learners for Language Paper assessment objectives that require grammatical commentary. The [subject knowledge audit](/pgce-subject-knowledge-audit-help/) section for grammar should honestly identify where explicit knowledge needs development — and the development plan should reference specific grammar-for-teaching resources.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Simple View of Reading and how do I use it in a PGCE English assignment?

The Simple View of Reading (Gough and Tunmer, 1986) is a theoretical framework that defines reading comprehension as the product of word recognition (decoding) and language comprehension. In PGCE English assignments, use it by connecting it to specific learner reading profiles — identifying which component is under-developed for specific learners in your placement class and evaluating the instructional implications. A learner who reads fluently but struggles with comprehension has adequate decoding but limited language comprehension — which may indicate vocabulary poverty or limited background knowledge. A learner who understands complex spoken text but reads slowly may have limited decoding fluency. The Simple View provides a diagnostic framework, not just a definition.

Do I need to reference the Ofsted English research review in PGCE assignments?

The Ofsted subject research reviews (published from 2020 onwards — the English research review was published in 2022) represent Ofsted's synthesis of the evidence base for English teaching. Referencing the English research review demonstrates awareness of current inspection-level thinking about the subject, which is professionally significant for trainees. However, the research review is an Ofsted document, not peer-reviewed academic research — it should be cited as an authoritative policy source (Ofsted, 2022) while primary academic research (Black and Wiliam, Myhill, Gough and Tunmer) remains the primary evidential foundation of academic essays.

How do I write about grammar teaching in a PGCE English assignment without just listing grammatical terms?

Grammar teaching in PGCE English assignments should be discussed in terms of pedagogy — how grammar is taught and why — rather than grammar itself. The key theoretical distinction is between decontextualised grammar instruction (labelling and parsing) and contextualised grammar instruction (Myhill et al., 2012 — examining grammatical choices and their effects in texts before applying them in writing). Evaluate whether the grammar teaching you observed or delivered in placement followed the contextualised approach and analyse the evidence about its impact on the writing quality you observed. This connects to TS3 (subject knowledge of how grammar is learned, not just what grammar is) and TS4 (well-structured lesson incorporating grammar within a meaningful writing context).
