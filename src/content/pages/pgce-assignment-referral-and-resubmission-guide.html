What Does It Mean When a PGCE Assignment Is Referred?

A PGCE assignment referral means the assignment has not met the minimum standard for a Pass at the first attempt — it has been assessed as failing to meet the Level 7 academic requirements of the programme, the specific module learning outcomes, or both. "Referred" is the standard term used at most PGCE providers; "failed" and "required to resubmit" are also used, depending on the institution's academic language.

A referral is not the same as withdrawal from the programme. A single referral — the first attempt that does not pass — is a standard academic event that most PGCE programmes allow for. Most providers allow one or two resubmission attempts before the assignment reaches a formal academic progression decision. The key is understanding precisely what was insufficient and resubmitting work that directly addresses the feedback.

Receiving a referral is distressing, particularly during a training year when placement pressures are already high. But the most counterproductive response is resubmitting quickly without fully understanding the feedback — which leads to a second referral that is far more serious for the trainee's academic progression. The most productive response is to read the feedback carefully, identify the specific gaps, seek tutorial support, and resubmit with targeted improvements. This guide covers each step of that process.

Common Reasons PGCE Assignments Are Referred by Assignment Type

Different PGCE assignment types have characteristic failure patterns. Knowing which failure pattern applies to your referral helps you target the resubmission accurately.

Reflective accounts (the most common assignment type): referrals almost always result from description rather than analysis. The trainee narrates what happened in a lesson — what activities were used, how pupils responded, what the outcome was — without explaining why things happened, what theory accounts for the pattern, or what the trainee will do differently next time. The Gibbs (1988) Analysis stage and the Kolb (1984) Abstract Conceptualisation stage are the stages where this failure most often occurs. [Reflective writing help](/pgce-reflective-writing-help/) covers the distinction between description and critical analysis in detail.

Pedagogy essays: referrals typically result from insufficient engagement with primary academic sources (relying on textbook summaries rather than original theorists); failure to connect theory to specific placement examples; or failure to evidence Teachers' Standards by number and sub-letter. Generic statements about "good teaching" rather than evidence-based analytical claims about specific pedagogical decisions are the characteristic signal of a pedagogy essay that will be referred.

Behaviour management assignments: referrals often result from describing behaviour management strategies without evaluating them critically — stating that "I used praise and reward because positive reinforcement works" rather than engaging with the evidence base, limitations, and alternative approaches. See [PGCE behaviour management assignment help](/pgce-behaviour-management-assignment-help/).

SEND and inclusion assignments: referrals result from policy description without critical engagement — citing the SEND Code of Practice (DfE & DoH, 2015) as authority without analysing its principles, or describing differentiation strategies without evaluating their effectiveness against theory. See [PGCE SEN and inclusion assignment help](/pgce-sen-and-inclusion-assignment-help/).

Curriculum theory essays: referrals result from describing curriculum models (Tyler, Stenhouse, Young) rather than critically evaluating and comparing them; or from failing to connect theoretical positions to specific examples from placement or subject context. See [PGCE curriculum design assignment help](/pgce-curriculum-design-assignment-help/).

How to Read PGCE Referral Feedback and Identify What Is Missing

PGCE referral feedback is often written in academic language that can be difficult to decode — particularly when you are upset about the grade. Reading it systematically, rather than emotionally, is the first step in producing a successful resubmission.

Step 1: Identify the specific assessment criteria that were not met. Most PGCE providers mark against published assessment criteria — typically covering academic writing quality, theoretical engagement, evidence from practice, critical analysis, and Teachers' Standards integration. The feedback should indicate which criteria were partially met or not met. If the feedback says "limited critical analysis," this is not just a general comment — it means the Analysis dimension of the marking criteria was not met, and that specific sections of the assignment need to be rewritten at a higher analytical level.

Step 2: Map the feedback to specific sections of your assignment. Identify which paragraphs or sections correspond to each piece of feedback. "Theory is summarised rather than applied" typically means the theoretical review sections are describing what Vygotsky said, not using Vygotsky to analyse what happened in a specific classroom. Mark each section of your submission with the relevant feedback point.

Step 3: Identify what a higher-level response would look like. "More critical engagement with the theory" means: engage with the theorist's claims, apply them to a specific example, evaluate their limitations, and connect them to Teachers' Standards. It does not mean add more theory names. Quality of engagement, not quantity of references, is the Level 7 requirement.

Step 4: Request a tutorial. Most PGCE providers allow referral students to request a tutorial with their module tutor before resubmission. Use this — not to ask "what should I write?" but to confirm that your reading of the feedback is correct: "I understand the feedback to mean X — is that right? I'm planning to address it by doing Y in sections 2 and 3." This check prevents a second referral caused by misreading the feedback.

PGCE Resubmission Rules: Timelines, Limits, and What Must Change

PGCE resubmission rules vary by provider — check your programme handbook and your referral notification letter for the specific terms at your institution. However, common patterns across most PGCE providers include the following.

Resubmission deadline: typically 4–8 weeks after the referral is issued. Some providers issue a fixed resubmission window (for example, all first-attempt referrals resubmit at the same date, regardless of when the original referral was issued). Do not assume the resubmission deadline is the same as the original deadline extended by four weeks — check the specific date on your referral notification.

Capped grades: resubmissions at most PGCE providers are capped at a Pass (40% or 50%, depending on the provider's grading scale). This means the resubmission cannot achieve a Merit or Distinction, regardless of how strong the resubmitted work is. This cap applies to the resubmission only — it does not affect the overall degree classification if your programme awards a graded PGCE.

What must change: the resubmission must make substantive improvements to the areas identified in the referral feedback. Resubmitting the same work with minor editing is the most common resubmission error — it will produce a second referral. All sections identified in the feedback as insufficient must be rewritten with higher analytical engagement, not just extended with additional material.

Second referral: a second referral on the same assignment is a serious academic progression issue at most providers. It triggers a formal meeting with the programme leader and may result in withdrawal from the module or from the programme. Some providers allow a supervised third attempt for exceptional circumstances. Most do not. The stakes of the second attempt are significantly higher than the first.

How to Improve a Referred PGCE Reflective Assignment

The most frequently referred assignment type is the reflective account. The most frequently cited cause of referral is "description rather than critical analysis." The most common resubmission error is adding more description, or adding more theory names, while leaving the analytical depth unchanged.

Improving a referred reflective assignment requires replacing descriptive paragraphs with analytical ones — which means rebuilding specific sections, not just editing them. A useful test: read each paragraph and ask "am I explaining what happened, or am I explaining why it happened and what theory accounts for it?" If the paragraph answers "what" — it is description. If it answers "why" plus "what theory" plus "what next" — it is analysis.

In Gibbs (1988) terms: the Description stage is necessary but must be brief (1–2 sentences). The Analysis stage must be the longest section of each reflective cycle — with named theorists, specific connections to the placement incident, and Teachers' Standards references. In Schön (1983) terms: Reflection-on-Action requires more than hindsight — it requires theoretical explanation of why the incident occurred as it did. Connecting it to TS8 (systematic reflection as professional responsibility) gives the analysis its standards-based framework.

PGCE Academic Appeals Process: When and How to Use It

A PGCE academic appeal is a formal challenge to an academic decision — including a referral grade — on procedural grounds. Appeals are not about disagreeing with a grade because you feel the work deserved better; they are about demonstrating that the assessment process was not conducted fairly or correctly.

Valid grounds for academic appeal at most UK universities: procedural irregularity (the assessment was not conducted in accordance with the stated procedure); extenuating circumstances that were not properly considered (illness, bereavement, or other serious life events that affected the first submission, not notified at the time); evidence of bias or unfair treatment in the marking process. Not valid grounds for appeal: "I worked very hard and deserved a better grade"; "I disagree with the marker's interpretation"; "other students with similar work passed."

Appeals process: consult your programme handbook for your institution's specific appeals process. Most require a formal written submission within a fixed period (typically 10–20 working days from the decision being communicated). Contact the Students' Union advice service — most universities provide independent academic advice for students considering appeals, which is free and confidential.

Important: lodging an appeal does not pause the resubmission deadline. You should submit your resubmission by the deadline regardless of whether you are also appealing the original decision.

How Many Referrals Can You Have Before Being Withdrawn From PGCE?

The number of referrals permitted before a formal progression decision is made varies by provider. Most PGCE providers allow: one referral per module (first attempt fails, one resubmission opportunity); a maximum of two or three module referrals across the full programme before a programme-level academic review is triggered. The specific limits are set out in your programme handbook under the sections on Academic Progression, Assessment Regulations, or Failure to Progress.

At some providers, a second referral on the same module (failing the resubmission) triggers immediate academic review with withdrawal as a likely outcome. At others, a second resubmission is possible under specific circumstances (medical evidence, extenuating circumstances). At no provider can referrals accumulate indefinitely — there is always a point at which the programme regulations require a progression decision, which may include withdrawal or transfer to a different exit qualification.

QTS implications: PGCE is a combination of academic qualification (the PGCE at Level 7) and professional recommendation for QTS. A trainee who fails academic modules may still be eligible for QTS if they meet the Teachers' Standards on placement, depending on the provider's programme structure. However, in practice, most PGCE providers require both academic and placement components to be passed before issuing a QTS recommendation. Check your programme regulations for the specific relationship between academic referrals and QTS eligibility at your institution.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will a PGCE referral show on my transcript?

The final transcript records the outcome of the module — if you pass on resubmission, the transcript typically records the capped grade (Pass) without explicitly stating it was a resubmission. The module appears as passed. However, practices vary by institution — some record "first attempt: fail, resubmission: pass" and others only record the final outcome. Check your provider's academic regulations for the specific policy on transcript entries for resubmissions. For most PGCE trainees, the transcript is less significant than the QTS recommendation and the academic reference provided by the programme leader — both of which inform future employment decisions.

Can I submit a referral resubmission as a completely new piece of writing?

It depends on the provider. Most PGCE programmes require resubmissions to be based on the original submission with targeted revisions — not a wholly new piece of writing, which would prevent the marker from verifying that the specific feedback has been addressed. Some providers allow the trainee to submit a substantially revised version that retains the original structure but rewrites the failed sections substantially. A small number of providers allow a fresh submission on the same topic. Check the resubmission guidelines issued with your referral notification — and if unclear, ask the module tutor in writing before starting.

What should I do if I receive a referral close to my placement deadline?

Notify your university programme leader and your placement school mentor immediately — both will need to know about the referral because it affects your academic and professional timeline. Request an extension to the resubmission deadline on grounds of placement commitments if the resubmission window overlaps directly with a placement period — most providers have provisions for this, but they must be formally requested. Do not attempt to write a resubmission while simultaneously managing a full-time placement without support. Contact your PGCE assignment support service for managed, prioritised help with the resubmission.
