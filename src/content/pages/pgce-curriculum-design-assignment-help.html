What Does a PGCE Curriculum Assignment Require?

A PGCE curriculum design or curriculum theory assignment requires the trainee to write academically about the design, sequencing, and philosophical basis of the curriculum — engaging with curriculum theory, subject-specific sequencing research, and the policy context of the National Curriculum in England. At Level 7, this means critically evaluating competing curriculum models, not describing government policy or listing subject content.

Curriculum assignments appear most frequently in [PGCE Secondary](/pgce-secondary-assignment-help/) programmes — where subject specialism makes curriculum design a substantive area of professional knowledge — and in [PGCE Further Education](/pgce-further-education-assignment-help/) programmes, where curriculum design is more explicitly part of the pedagogical role. [PGCE Primary](/pgce-primary-assignment-help/) trainees may encounter curriculum theory essays, but they are less common than in Secondary contexts.

Teachers' Standards connections: TS3 (Demonstrate good subject and curriculum knowledge), TS4 (Plan and teach well-structured lessons — including planning for sequencing within a unit of work), and TS2 (Promote good progress — which requires understanding of how the curriculum develops learner knowledge over time). The most advanced curriculum essays also engage with Ofsted's Education Inspection Framework (EIF, 2019) — specifically its framing of curriculum as "intent, implementation, and impact" — which provides a policy-level structure for curriculum analysis.

Assignment word counts vary: curriculum design essays at PGCE Secondary level are typically 2,500–4,000 words. Some providers also require a curriculum planning artefact — a medium-term plan or sequence of lessons — alongside the analytical essay.

Curriculum Theory for PGCE: Tyler, Stenhouse, and Young's Powerful Knowledge

Three curriculum theorists form the foundation of PGCE curriculum essays: Tyler (1949), Stenhouse (1975), and Young (2008, 2013). Each represents a fundamentally different view of what a curriculum is, what it is for, and how it should be designed — and the tensions between them provide the analytical engine for Level 7 curriculum essays.

Tyler (1949) — the objectives model. Ralph Tyler's Basic Principles of Curriculum and Instruction proposed that curriculum design should begin with objectives (what outcomes are to be achieved), then determine experiences (what learning activities achieve those outcomes), then organise those experiences (how they are sequenced), then evaluate whether the objectives are met. Tyler's model is product-focused, technicist, and linear — the curriculum is designed backwards from predetermined outcomes. It underpins much of the National Curriculum's assessment framework and Ofsted's outcomes-focused inspection approach. Critique at Level 7: Tyler's model assumes that educational outcomes can be pre-specified and measured, which is contested for complex human capacities like critical thinking, aesthetic response, and character development.

Stenhouse (1975) — the process model. Lawrence Stenhouse's An Introduction to Curriculum Research and Development proposed an alternative: curriculum as the process of inquiry — the teacher as researcher, the classroom as a site of curriculum development. Stenhouse rejected Tyler's pre-specified objectives, arguing that meaningful educational experiences cannot be fully anticipated in advance. The process model values the quality of the educational encounter, not just the measurable outcome. It connects to Dewey's (1916) view of education as growth through experience and to Schön's (1983) Reflective Practitioner — the teacher who inquires into practice is the curriculum developer. Critique: the process model is difficult to scale and to evaluate; it provides limited guidance for trainees who need a systematic planning framework.

Young (2008, 2013) — Powerful Knowledge. Michael Young's Bringing Knowledge Back In (2008) and Knowledge and the Future School (2013, with Muller) propose that the primary purpose of schooling is to give learners access to "powerful knowledge" — the systematic, theoretical, discipline-based knowledge that learners do not encounter in everyday experience and cannot acquire informally. Powerful knowledge is conceptually rich, generalising, and transformative — it gives learners the tools to think about the world in ways that exceed common sense. For PGCE curriculum essays, Young's framework provides a rationale for subject-centred, knowledge-rich curriculum design — and a critique of curriculum approaches that prioritise skills, relevance, or learner-centredness at the expense of disciplinary knowledge.

Knowledge-Rich Curriculum in PGCE Assignments: Hirsch and the National Curriculum

E.D. Hirsch (1988) — Cultural Literacy — argued that shared background knowledge is the foundation of reading comprehension and cultural participation. Learners who lack the common cultural knowledge that literate adults take for granted are disadvantaged in school and society. Hirsch's "knowledge-rich" curriculum framework — developed into Core Knowledge curricula in the US and adopted by some UK multi-academy trusts — sequences specific knowledge explicitly, teaches it directly, and assesses whether learners have retained it. It aligns closely with Young's Powerful Knowledge framework and with the current Ofsted EIF emphasis on curriculum as substantive and cumulative knowledge building.

The National Curriculum for England (DfE, 2014) occupies an ambiguous position between knowledge-rich and competence-based curriculum models. It specifies programme of study content (knowledge requirements) but also includes process elements (developing skills, applying methods, communicating findings). PGCE curriculum essays that identify and analyse this tension within the National Curriculum — rather than treating it as a unified, unproblematic policy document — demonstrate the critical engagement with curriculum policy that Level 7 assessment requires.

Connection to Ofsted's EIF (2019): the framework's concept of "curriculum intent" (what the school intends for learners to know, understand, and be able to do) maps closely to knowledge-rich curriculum models. Ofsted's research reviews (published from 2020 onwards for each subject) provide subject-specific curriculum analysis that PGCE Secondary trainees can engage with — and that demonstrate the professional currency of curriculum theory knowledge.

Bruner's Spiral Curriculum in PGCE Subject Planning Assignments

Bruner (1960) — The Process of Education — proposed that any subject can be taught effectively in some intellectually honest form to any stage of development. The spiral curriculum revisits core concepts and principles at increasing depth and complexity as learners progress through school — returning to foundational ideas with greater sophistication each time they are encountered. This is in direct contrast to a linear curriculum that teaches topics sequentially without return.

For PGCE curriculum design assignments, Bruner's spiral curriculum provides a theoretical rationale for sequencing decisions: why core concepts (narrative structure in English; number sense in Mathematics; fair testing in Science) are revisited across year groups with increasing demand, rather than taught once and moved on from. Secondary PGCE trainees writing about medium-term planning or subject sequencing can apply the spiral curriculum model to justify the vertical coherence (how learning builds across years) of their subject's curriculum.

Bruner (1966) also proposed three modes of representation — enactive (learning through action), iconic (learning through images), and symbolic (learning through language and notation) — which provides a micro-level sequencing framework within individual lessons and units: new concepts should be introduced enactively and iconically before symbolic notation is introduced. This connects directly to [lesson design](/pgce-lesson-study-assignment-help/) and [PGCE Primary](/pgce-primary-assignment-help/) curriculum planning contexts.

How to Critically Evaluate Competing Curriculum Models in PGCE

Critical evaluation of curriculum models in PGCE assignments means engaging with the philosophical and political assumptions embedded in each model — not just describing what each model proposes. Curriculum theory is not politically neutral: the choice of which knowledge to include, whose knowledge is valued, and how progress through the curriculum is measured reflects power relations in society.

The tension between knowledge-rich (Tyler, Young, Hirsch) and process-based (Stenhouse, Dewey) models is the central debate in curriculum studies. Knowledge-rich models are critiqued for reproducing dominant cultural values and marginalising the knowledge traditions of communities not represented in the Western academic canon. Process models are critiqued for leaving learners from disadvantaged backgrounds without the cultural capital that knowledge-rich curricula provide to all. PGCE curriculum essays that engage with this tension — rather than advocating straightforwardly for one position — demonstrate the critical thinking that Level 7 assessment requires.

A Level 7 critical evaluation paragraph: "Young's (2008) Powerful Knowledge framework offers a social justice rationale for knowledge-rich curriculum design — it argues that systematic disciplinary knowledge is the tool that enables all learners, regardless of background, to think beyond their immediate experience. However, critics — notably Youdell (2011) — argue that 'powerful knowledge' typically reflects the knowledge traditions of dominant groups, and that a curriculum designed around it may reproduce rather than challenge existing inequalities. In my placement school's History curriculum, the decision to include Black British history and postcolonial perspectives alongside the traditional British political narrative represents an attempt to construct a curriculum that is both knowledge-rich and culturally representative — a position consistent with the principle that Powerful Knowledge should be critically selected, not uncritically inherited."

Subject Sequencing and Curriculum Design in PGCE Secondary Assignments

Subject sequencing — the order in which concepts, topics, and skills are introduced across a scheme of work and across year groups — is one of the most practically significant aspects of curriculum design for PGCE Secondary trainees. Good sequencing builds on prior knowledge, introduces complexity gradually, and creates the conditions for deep understanding rather than surface coverage.

Theories that inform sequencing decisions in PGCE curriculum essays: Ausubel's (1968) advance organiser principle — new learning is assimilated more effectively when prior knowledge is activated first; Gagné's (1965) learning hierarchies — some concepts must precede others logically, not just pedagogically; Bruner's (1960) spiral curriculum — core concepts should be revisited with increasing sophistication. For [PGCE Secondary Maths](/pgce-secondary-maths-assignment-help/) trainees, the NCETM (National Centre for Excellence in the Teaching of Mathematics) curriculum frameworks provide sequencing guidance grounded in mastery research. For [PGCE Secondary English](/pgce-secondary-english-assignment-help/) trainees, Counsell's (2018) work on the relationship between substantive and disciplinary knowledge in History provides a model for thinking about knowledge sequencing that applies across humanities subjects.

Hidden Curriculum and Educational Philosophy in PGCE Essays

The hidden curriculum — the values, norms, and social hierarchies that are transmitted through schooling without being explicitly taught — is an important concept for PGCE curriculum essays that engage with the sociological dimensions of education. Jackson (1968) coined the term; Apple (1979) developed it as a critique of how schools reproduce dominant ideologies through selection of content, teacher-pupil relationships, and assessment regimes.

For PGCE essays, the hidden curriculum is most relevant in discussions of educational purpose: what are schools for? If the explicit curriculum (what is taught) promotes critical thinking and democratic citizenship, but the hidden curriculum (how school is organised) rewards compliance, passivity, and deference to authority, the gap between the two creates a contradictory educational experience. Assignments that address this gap — particularly in the context of Freire's (1970) critique of "banking" education (in which knowledge is deposited into passive learners) versus dialogue-based education — engage with the philosophical foundations of curriculum at a level appropriate to Level 7.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between curriculum intent, implementation, and impact in PGCE assignments?

These three terms come from Ofsted's Education Inspection Framework (EIF, 2019). Curriculum intent refers to the school's design decisions about what learners will know, understand, and be able to do — the "what" and "why" of the curriculum. Curriculum implementation refers to how the intended curriculum is taught — the "how" at lesson and unit level. Curriculum impact refers to what learners have actually learned — the outcome evidence. For PGCE curriculum assignments, this framework provides a useful structure: evaluate the intended curriculum of your placement school or subject department (intent), analyse how the curriculum is taught in practice (implementation), and assess what evidence exists about its effect on learners (impact). This connects directly to Tyler's objectives model and provides a policy-level vocabulary for curriculum analysis.

Do I need to engage with Michael Young's Powerful Knowledge in a PGCE curriculum essay?

Young's Powerful Knowledge framework (2008, 2013) is one of the most cited curriculum frameworks in current PGCE marking criteria — particularly at providers that align with an Ofsted-focused knowledge-rich curriculum agenda. If your assignment brief refers to "knowledge-rich curriculum," "curriculum design," or "curriculum intent," Young is likely an expected reference. However, the most sophisticated essays engage with critiques of Powerful Knowledge (Youdell, 2011; Reay, 2012) as well as its arguments — rather than citing Young as authoritative without critical engagement.

What is the spiral curriculum and how do I use it in a PGCE assignment?

Bruner's (1960) spiral curriculum proposes that core concepts should be revisited across years of schooling at increasing depth — returning to the same ideas with greater complexity as learners develop. In PGCE assignments, the spiral curriculum justifies sequencing decisions that revisit fundamental ideas rather than treating topics as one-time events. To use it analytically: identify a core concept in your subject, show how it appears in progressively more complex forms across year groups (e.g., narrative structure in KS2, KS3, and KS4 English; number sense in Primary and Secondary Maths), and evaluate whether your placement school's curriculum sequence reflects spiral principles or treats topics as isolated units.
