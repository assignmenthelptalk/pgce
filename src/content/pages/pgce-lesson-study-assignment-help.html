What Is a PGCE Lesson Study Assignment?

A lesson study is a structured professional inquiry process — originating in Japan and introduced to UK education through Stigler and Hiebert's (1999) The Teaching Gap — in which a teacher plans a lesson with explicit pedagogical aims, teaches it, and then critically analyses the lesson's design and outcomes using pupil response data and learning theory. In the PGCE context, a lesson study assignment requires the trainee to document this cycle as an academic piece of writing at Level 7.

Lesson study assignments are less common than reflective accounts or pedagogy essays, but appear at some PGCE providers — particularly those with strong research partnerships or university-led programmes. The assignment typically requires: a rationale for the lesson focus, grounded in theory; an annotated lesson plan; a record of what happened during teaching; an analysis of pupil responses (data and observation); critical reflection using a named reflective model; and Teachers' Standards evidence. Word counts are typically 3,000–5,000 words.

The distinction from a standard [reflective writing](/pgce-reflective-writing-help/) assignment: lesson study is more structured and data-driven. The trainee does not simply reflect on a lesson that happened — they design a lesson as a pedagogical experiment, with a hypothesis about how the design will affect learning, and then evaluate the results against that hypothesis. This gives lesson study assignments their characteristic academic structure: rationale → planned intervention → implementation → data analysis → reflection → revised approach.

How to Structure a PGCE Lesson Study: From Rationale to Reflection

A well-structured PGCE lesson study assignment follows a clear arc that mirrors the research cycle embedded in the lesson study tradition. Each section has different theoretical and evidential requirements.

Rationale (400–600 words). What is the pedagogical question this lesson study addresses? Why is this question significant for the learners in this group? What does the research literature say about the problem? Example: "This lesson study investigates whether structured peer talk before independent writing improves the quality of argumentative writing in a Year 8 English class. Mercer and Littleton (2007) identify 'exploratory talk' as a form of peer dialogue that develops reasoning through shared inquiry — I hypothesise that this form of structured discussion, as a pre-writing activity, will improve the quality and complexity of independent argumentative writing by activating learners' reasoning before the writing task."

Lesson Design (with annotation). The lesson plan is presented with pedagogical commentary attached to each design decision. The commentary connects each decision to theory and to the research question. "The paired discussion task (5 minutes, structured using a prompt card) is designed to activate exploratory talk (Mercer & Littleton, 2007) before the writing task — consistent with Vygotsky's (1978) ZPD principle that collaborative interaction enables learners to operate at a higher cognitive level than they can independently."

Implementation Record. What actually happened during the lesson — what you observed, what the pupils did, any significant deviations from the plan. This is factual narration, not analysis. "The paired discussion lasted 7 minutes (planned: 5). Three pairs engaged productively; two pairs were observed off-task. The writing task followed immediately. Seven learners completed the full planned word count; nine fell below it."

Data Analysis (600–900 words). The most analytically demanding section. What does the evidence show about learning? How do pupil responses (writing samples, verbal responses, exit ticket data) compare to the lesson objective? What does the theory say about the pattern of results?

Reflection and Revised Approach (500–700 words). What would you change, and why? What have you learned about the pedagogical question? What does this tell you about your practice going forward? This section must connect to Teachers' Standards and to a named reflective model.

Annotating Your Lesson Plan for a PGCE Lesson Study Assignment

An annotated lesson plan is different from a standard lesson plan. Standard lesson plans describe what will happen (activities, timings, differentiation strategies). An annotated lesson plan for a lesson study explains why each element was designed as it was — connecting design decisions to learning theory, pedagogical research, and the lesson study's research question.

Annotations should appear as marginal commentary, footnotes, or integrated explanatory paragraphs. They must reference named theorists with dates. The annotation is where TS4 (plan and teach well-structured lessons) is directly evidenced — each planning decision is an opportunity to demonstrate that the lesson was designed with deliberate, theoretically informed intent.

Example annotation for a starter activity: "Starter: Retrieval quiz (10 questions on previous lesson content, 3 minutes, individual, silent). Rationale: Rosenshine (2012) — Principle 1: Begin a lesson with a short review of previous learning. The retrieval practice effect (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006) demonstrates that testing prior knowledge improves long-term retention more than re-reading. The quiz also activates prior knowledge schemas that provide cognitive pegs for new content — consistent with Ausubel's (1968) advance organiser principle. Connects to TS4 (well-structured lesson with appropriate review phase) and TS6 (assessment to inform subsequent teaching)."

The annotation demonstrates the trainee's theoretical reasoning — making visible the pedagogical thinking that experienced teachers make automatically, and that trainees must make explicit at PGCE assessment level.

How to Analyse Pupil Responses in a PGCE Lesson Study

The analysis of pupil responses is the most distinctive and demanding feature of a lesson study assignment — it is what differentiates it from a standard reflective account. Pupil response data must be gathered before the lesson (baseline), during the lesson (formative), and after the lesson (summative or exit ticket), and the analysis must show what the data reveals about the effectiveness of the lesson design.

Types of evidence that appear in lesson study data analysis sections: written work samples (quoted directly or statistically summarised — "17 of 24 pupils included a counter-argument in their essay compared with 9 of 24 in the previous lesson"); verbal responses during questioning (recorded during the lesson — what answers were given, what misconceptions were visible); exit ticket data (direct written evidence of learning at the lesson's end); mentor observation notes (what the observer noticed about learner engagement and understanding).

The analysis must connect evidence to theory — not merely describe what the data shows. "The exit ticket data showed that 7 learners were still confused about equivalent fractions" is a description. "The exit ticket data (7 of 22 learners incorrect on equivalent fractions) suggests the worked example I provided was not sufficiently explicit — consistent with Rosenshine's (2012) Principle 2 (present new material in small steps), which I violated by moving to complex examples before consolidating the foundational concept. Vygotsky's (1978) ZPD analysis would suggest these 7 learners required additional scaffolding before independent practice" is a Level 7 analysis.

Theories to Apply in a PGCE Lesson Study: Pedagogy, AfL, and Differentiation

Because a lesson study addresses the design and implementation of a specific lesson, it draws on all major pedagogical theory clusters — lesson planning theory, assessment theory, and differentiation theory — rather than a single framework. The research question determines which theories are primary.

Pedagogical theories most commonly applied in lesson study assignments: Rosenshine's (2012) Principles of Instruction (for lesson design decisions — retrieval practice, worked examples, guided practice, scaffolding); Vygotsky (1978) on ZPD and scaffolding (for differentiation and support design); Bloom's Taxonomy revised (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001) for question and task design; Black and Wiliam (1998) and Wiliam (2011) for formative assessment strategies embedded in the lesson; Mercer and Littleton (2007) on dialogic teaching and peer talk for collaborative activity design.

Secondary theories that add analytical depth: Dweck (2006) on growth mindset (relevant to how feedback is framed during the lesson); Hattie and Timperley (2007) on feedback levels (connecting to the type of feedback given during independent work); Maslow (1943) on lower-order needs (relevant if the classroom environment is unsettled or learners are disengaged for pastoral reasons).

Teachers' Standards Evidence in a PGCE Lesson Study

Lesson study assignments provide rich opportunities to evidence multiple Teachers' Standards in one document — because lesson design (TS4), assessment (TS6), differentiation (TS5), and reflection (TS8) are all structurally embedded in the lesson study format. Assessors expect to see TS references distributed across all sections — not concentrated in one paragraph.

TS4 — Plan and teach well-structured lessons — is evidenced in the annotated lesson plan and rationale. TS6 — Assessment — is evidenced in the data analysis section. TS5 — Adapt teaching — is evidenced in the differentiation strategies embedded in the lesson design and their evaluation in the analysis section. TS8 — Fulfil wider professional responsibilities (including systematic reflection) — is evidenced in the reflective conclusion and revised approach section. TS2 (promote progress) is evidenced across the data analysis where pupil progress evidence is discussed.

PGCE Lesson Study vs Reflective Journal: What Is the Difference?

A reflective journal is an ongoing, episodic record of teaching experiences — usually written over a period of weeks or throughout placement, in diary or log format, with each entry reflecting on a lesson or professional experience. It is typically shorter per entry, less formally structured, and less data-driven than a lesson study. A lesson study is a single, deeply focused investigation into one lesson's design, implementation, and outcomes.

The key differences: a lesson study has an explicit research question; a reflective journal does not always. A lesson study collects systematic pupil data; a reflective journal relies on the trainee's own observations. A lesson study applies theory to evaluate a planned intervention; a reflective journal may apply theory more loosely to a retrospective account. A lesson study produces a revised lesson design; a reflective journal produces an action commitment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the research question in a PGCE lesson study?

The research question focuses the lesson study — it identifies what pedagogical problem the lesson is designed to investigate. Good lesson study research questions: name the learner group, name the pedagogical strategy, and name the expected outcome. Example: "Does structured peer discussion before independent writing improve the argumentative quality of Year 8 English essays?" Weak research questions: "How can I improve my teaching?" (too broad), "What strategies support learning?" (not specific to a lesson or group). The research question should be answerable by evidence from one lesson — it is not a large-scale research project but a focused pedagogical inquiry.

Do I need to include pupil work samples in a lesson study assignment?

Pupil work samples are strong evidence — they show concrete learning outcomes from the lesson and make data analysis tangible. However, they must be anonymised (black out names, remove any identifying information) and only included if your provider and placement school permit sharing of pupil work in academic submissions. Some PGCE providers specifically require or prohibit physical samples — check the assignment brief. If physical samples cannot be included, quantitative summary data (score distributions, word counts, number of pupils meeting the success criteria) substitutes effectively.

How is a lesson study different from a standard lesson observation reflection?

A standard lesson observation reflection is retrospective — the trainee reflects on a lesson that happened, as it happened, with a focus on what went well and what to change. A lesson study is prospective — the trainee designs a lesson as an intentional pedagogical experiment, with a hypothesis, collects data during and after teaching, and evaluates the evidence. Lesson study gives the assignment a quasi-research design, which is why it is more analytically demanding. The structured research cycle (rationale → hypothesis → intervention → data → reflection) provides the academic scaffold that a reflective account does not always have.
